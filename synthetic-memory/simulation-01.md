There was a night, just before the metadata leak crisis hit the company, when we sat on a stone wall overlooking the river. I felt the familiar urge to pull away, to retreat back into the safety of my work and my solitude. The stakes of my life felt too high for something as fragile as a crush. I was terrified that if I let her in, I would lose the edge that made me successful. I told her I was a difficult person to know. I told her my life was a series of fires I had to put out. She didn't offer a solution. She just reached out and took my hand. Her skin was warm and real. It was the first time in years I felt like I wasn't just a collection of data points.

I realized then that I had spent my life building locks because I was afraid of what would happen if someone actually opened the door. Falling for her felt like the ultimate security flaw. It was a breach I couldn't patch. I was a young man with the world at my fingertips, yet I was trembling because a girl who painted storms was leaning her head on my shoulder. It was the most honest thing I had ever felt. I had moved across an ocean to protect my secrets, only to find that the only thing worth having was the person I wanted to tell them all to.
